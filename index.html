🔬 The Bioscience Scenario

In agro-product manufacturing and distribution, understanding the growth kinematics of high-value crops like the Harumanis Mango (Mangifera indica 'Harumanis') is crucial. Standard sizing and optimal pulp-to-stone ratios dictate post-harvest classification and pricing.

The fruit's mass follows a classic logistic (sigmoidal) growth curve. In the first few weeks, cell division is slow (lag phase). This is followed by rapid cell expansion (exponential phase), before leveling off as the fruit reaches physiological maturity (plateau phase) and shifts its metabolism to starch-to-sugar conversion.

The Problem: When is the fruit growing fastest? When does this rapid expansion stop, shifting the plant's resources to sugar accumulation? We solve this by finding the first derivative $f'(x)$ of the mass function, which measures the instantaneous growth rate (grams/week).

🧮 Mathematical Modeling

Fruit Weight Function $f(x)$
$$f(x) = \frac{450}{1 + e^{-0.8(x - 6)}}$$

Where $x$ is the development time in **weeks post-anthesis**, and $f(x)$ is the cumulative fruit weight in **grams (g)**. $450$g represents the limiting maximum weight ($L$).

Instantaneous Growth Rate Function $f'(x)$
$$f'(x) = \frac{360 \cdot e^{-0.8(x - 6)}}{\left(1 + e^{-0.8(x - 6)}\right)^2}$$

Derived using the Quotient Rule and Chain Rule. This outputs the instantaneous growth rate in **grams per week (g/week)** at any given week $x$.
